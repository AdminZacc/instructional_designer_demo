Assessment · Evaluation Design

Skills Gap Analysis — Sales Team

A Kirkpatrick Level 2 & 3 evaluation plan built to surface real performance gaps — not just quiz scores. Paired with scenario-based assessment items that mirror actual sales conversations and tracked via Tableau dashboards.

Tools: Excel, SurveyMonkey, Tableau Format: Mixed-method evaluation Audience: 47-person sales org

Project Snapshot

Problem

Sales training was measured almost exclusively by completion rates and post-quiz scores. Leadership had no visibility into whether reps could actually apply new skills in live deals — and conversion rates reflected it.

Design Goal

Build an evaluation architecture that distinguishes learning (L2) from behavior change (L3), using scenario-based items that require application — not recognition — and connect training outputs to business data.

Outcome

22-point increase in scenario assessment scores post-training. Manager observation data confirmed 78% of reps applied target behaviors on the floor within 45 days. Tableau dashboard now part of the quarterly L&D review.

Evaluation Framework

The plan targets two Kirkpatrick levels. Level 1 (satisfaction) was already tracked — the gap was in knowing whether training actually moved the needle.

L1

Reaction

Already tracked via end-of-course satisfaction surveys. Not in scope for this project.

L2

Learning

Did reps acquire the target knowledge and skills? Measured via scenario-based pre/post assessment items scored against a rubric.

  • SurveyMonkey scenario assessments (pre & post)
  • Excel scoring rubric with weighted criteria
  • Cohort-level gain score analysis
L3

Behavior

Are reps applying new skills on the floor 30–60 days post-training? Measured via structured manager observation and CRM data pulls.

  • Manager observation checklist (standardized)
  • CRM activity data (30 / 45 / 60-day checkpoints)
  • Tableau dashboard for ongoing monitoring
L4

Results

Business impact (quota attainment, win rate). Planned for Phase 2 once L3 baselines are established.

Scenario-Based Assessment — Sample Item

These items move beyond recall. Each scenario presents a realistic sales moment and requires the learner to choose the most effective response — scored against a four-point rubric.

Assessment Item — Level 2 · Scenario

Handling the "We Already Have a Vendor" Objection

—

SITUATION

You're 12 minutes into a discovery call with a mid-market prospect. The conversation has been going well — they've confirmed budget authority and acknowledged a pain point. Then the contact says:

"Look, we appreciate your time, but we've been with our current vendor for six years. Switching is a huge lift for our team and honestly not something we're looking at right now."

What is the most effective next move?

Select one response

Scoring Rubric

A
1 / 4 Passive — misses diagnostic opportunity
B
4 / 4 Best — surfaces real objection before countering
C
2 / 4 Partial — skips listening, leads with pitch
D
1 / 4 Premature — assumes readiness that was not confirmed

Design Decisions

01

Scenario over recall

Multiple-choice recall items inflate scores without predicting behavior. Every assessment item places the learner inside a real sales conversation — they must choose a response, not a definition.

02

Weighted rubric scoring

Rather than binary correct/wrong, a four-point rubric captures degree of application. This produces richer data for cohort analysis and flags learners who are close — not just those who failed.

03

Pre/post design for gain scores

Administering the same scenario set before and after training isolates the effect of the intervention. Gain scores prevent high prior-knowledge learners from skewing overall results and expose where training added the least lift.

04

L3 tied to observable behavior, not self-report

Manager observation checklists — not self-assessments — anchor the L3 measurement. Calibration sessions ensured inter-rater reliability across 11 managers before data collection began.
