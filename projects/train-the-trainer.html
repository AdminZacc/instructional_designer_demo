ILT · Facilitation Design

Train-the-Trainer Workshop

A full facilitation package — slide deck, facilitator guide, participant workbook, and post-training survey — designed to build internal training capacity across a distributed organization. Trainers rated their confidence to deliver at 4.6 / 5 after a single two-day session.

Tools: PowerPoint, MS Forms Format: 2-day ILT workshop Audience: Internal subject-matter experts

Project Snapshot

Problem

A growing organization had deep subject-matter expertise sitting in silos — but no structured way to transfer that knowledge. Ad-hoc "shadow shifts" weren't producing consistent trainers, and onboarding quality varied wildly by region.

Design Goal

Equip a cohort of 18 internal SMEs to deliver a standardized onboarding experience. The goal wasn't just content mastery — it was building facilitation skill, handling participant pushback, and managing group dynamics.

Outcome

18 certified internal trainers deployed across 5 regional offices. New-hire onboarding time-to-proficiency dropped by 34% over two quarters. The facilitator guide was adopted as the organization's standard training template.

Package Deliverables

Four interlocking documents — each designed to work independently and together. An SME could pick up the facilitator guide on day one and run the session without additional coaching.

01

Slide Deck

80-slide PowerPoint built for trainer use — not for reading aloud. Each slide pairs a minimal visual with a "say / do / ask" note in the speaker pane. Animations are intentional, not decorative.

  • Modular structure — sections drop in or out by cohort
  • Branded master template with accessibility-compliant contrast
  • Facilitator cue annotations on 40% of slides
02

Facilitator Guide

A 60-page script-free guide organized by session segment. Each segment includes learning objectives, estimated time, facilitation moves, and anticipated participant questions with suggested responses.

  • Segment-level timing with buffer notes
  • Pushback scripts for 12 common objections
  • Differentiation flags for mixed-experience cohorts
03

Participant Workbook

A 28-page print-ready workbook that follows the session arc. Guided notes, application exercises, and reflection prompts are sequenced so participants leave with a ready-to-use reference, not a binder of slides.

  • Guided note scaffolds (not full transcripts)
  • 3 practice scenario exercises with coaching rubrics
  • Personal action-planning template on final page
04

Post-Training Survey

A 12-item MS Forms survey measuring Kirkpatrick L1 (satisfaction) and L2 confidence — not just "did you like it," but "could you do it." Data fed directly into the L&D team's quarterly review.

  • Likert + open-response hybrid format
  • Skill-confidence self-ratings across 6 facilitation competencies
  • Auto-report via MS Forms analytics dashboard

Transition buffer. Facilitator resets the room and pre-loads the next activity. Remind participants of the 15-minute window — late returners disrupt momentum.

Design Decisions

01

Script-free facilitator guide

A word-for-word script creates readers, not facilitators. The guide uses segment intentions, key questions, and facilitation moves — giving SMEs enough structure to feel confident without locking them into someone else's voice.

02

Micro-teach with structured feedback

Reading about facilitation doesn't build facilitation skill. Two full rounds of micro-teaching — with rubric-based peer feedback — ensure participants practice the actual behavior being developed, not just discuss it.

03

Workbook as field reference, not note-taking log

Participant workbooks are often just printed slides. This one is structured so that someone who missed the session could pick it up and run a segment — making it a durable performance support tool, not a souvenir.

04

Survey measures confidence, not just satisfaction

The post-training survey includes self-rated confidence across six facilitation competencies — a leading indicator of whether trainers will actually use what they learned, distinct from whether they enjoyed the session.
